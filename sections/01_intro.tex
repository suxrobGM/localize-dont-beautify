Surgeons have used simulated outcomes in patient consultations for decades.
Computer imaging has been associated with improved rhinoplasty patient
satisfaction~\cite{sharp2002computerimaging}, and how closely a 3D simulation
matches the eventual outcome predicts how satisfied the patient will
be~\cite{yamamichi2025crisalix}. The same literature warns that an over-idealized
preview sets expectations no operation can meet~\cite{agarwal2007morph}. Producing
these previews today requires either manual morphing skill or dedicated 3D
systems; meanwhile, general-purpose image-editing models served over public APIs
can perform instruction-guided photo edits. This motivates testing whether those
services can support controlled cosmetic-surgery visualization without access to
their weights or inference process.

In preliminary tests, a request for a specific visible change, such as jawline
and neck repositioning or dorsal-hump reduction, also altered skin, lighting, and
other facial regions. Such off-target change can both weaken identity retention
and present cosmetic effects unrelated to the requested procedure. The
training-free
localization literature knows how to confine
edits~\cite{avrahami2022blended,couairon2023diffedit}, but every such method needs
the model's latents, attention, or sampling loop, and a hosted API exposes none of
them.

We therefore evaluate how much off-target change can be prevented on the client.
The pilot benchmark covers six editing configurations (GPT Image~2 at two quality
tiers, Nano Banana~Pro and~2, Seedream~5.0 Lite, and FLUX.2~[pro]) plus a Qwen
inpainting endpoint, two requested edit types, and publicly accessible
before/after photograph pairs. A common random seed was sent with every request,
though two endpoints ignore it (Sec.~\ref{sec:ladder}), and each matrix cell
contains one generated output. Our
contributions are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A model-agnostic control ladder.} Three rungs over the same
  black-box endpoints: prompt-only, a client-side masked composite
  (landmark-derived region masks, alignment, feathered blending, pose gating),
  and native masked inpainting where supported. The composite adapts the final
  step of blended-diffusion editing to a setting where nothing inside the model
  is reachable (Sec.~\ref{sec:method}).
  \item \textbf{A two-part diagnostic protocol.} ArcFace identity is paired with
  a CIELAB region-localization ratio and its target/off-target components. The
  metrics detect identity loss, off-target pixel change, and near-copies, but do
  not measure clinical plausibility. Postoperative photographs provide a separate
  identity reference, and a surgeon rubric is released for future validation
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:protocol}).
  \item \textbf{A pilot benchmark.} Client-side compositing enforces off-mask
  preservation across the tested editors without materially eliminating on-target
  pixel change. The editors differ in edit strength and identity retention, while
  the one tested mask-specific endpoint does not outperform compositing. A
  two-order, single-face chain is reported only as a feasibility probe
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{itemize}

To our knowledge, prior work has not compared this client-side control ladder
across multiple hosted black-box editors in cosmetic-surgery preview tasks. The
scope is narrow: bespoke surgical simulators and composited rhinoplasty systems
exist~\cite{knoedler2024rhinoplastygan,agarwal2026envisage}, and hosted editors
have been benchmarked in other domains~\cite{qian2025giebench,gptimgeval2025}.
Our contribution is the hosted-API comparison and controlled intervention, not a
new compositing algorithm, clinical outcome model, or clinical validation study
(Sec.~\ref{sec:related}).\footnote{Analysis code and paper source:
\url{https://github.com/suxrobGM/localize-dont-beautify}}
