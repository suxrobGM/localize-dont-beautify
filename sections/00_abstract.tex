Hosted image-editing models can follow the instruction ``perform a deep plane
facelift'' --- but they edit the whole face, returning a beauty-filter rendition
that misrepresents both the patient and the operation. We benchmark four
off-the-shelf hosted editors (GPT Image~2, Seedream~5.0, Qwen-Image-Edit) on three
facial procedures using real before/after photograph pairs, under a control
ladder that requires no model access: prompt-only, a client-side masked composite
built from facial landmarks, and native masked inpainting. An automated protocol
scores each edit for the two opposing failure modes --- identity drift and
off-target change --- via ArcFace cosine and a CIELAB region-localization ratio,
with real post-operative photographs as a sanity check. Masked compositing pins
localization at a median of \MedLocComposite{} across models, where prompt-only
control reaches \MedLocPromptOnly{} and, unexpectedly, the mask-accepting
endpoint under-edits (localization \MedLocInpaint{} with near-unity identity);
identity stays above the 0.6 acceptance floor in all \NumScoredEdits{} scored
edits, and two-procedure chains compose in either order. The results locate the
capability gap for surgical previews in \emph{control} rather than in the models,
and show the control can live entirely on the client.
