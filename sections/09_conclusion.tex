\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this pilot, text instructions alone did not prevent hosted image editors
from changing a predefined facial keep zone. A landmark-derived mask,
alignment, and client-side feathered composite reduced that measured off-target
change while retaining similar target-region pixel change, uniformly across the
six editors tested and at a median provider cost of \$\MedCost{} per image. The
tested Qwen inpainting endpoint did not outperform that post-hoc control, but
one endpoint cannot support a general conclusion about masked inpainting.

The study does not validate cosmetic-surgery outcome prediction, and
client-side compositing should be read as a control for off-target pixel
changes, not as evidence that a preview is clinically accurate or safe for
patient counseling. The main analysis contains \NumPrimaryFaces{} faces, one
generated output per cell, no surgeon ratings, limited profile support, and no
metric of improvement over the original image relative to postoperative
anatomy. The next study should add repeated generations, pose-robust
whole-image localization, input-relative postoperative metrics, multiple
blinded surgeons with inter-rater agreement, a larger demographically
characterized cohort, documented image-use authorization, and immutable or
self-hosted model baselines. What this pilot does establish is the systems
point: for editors that expose no internals, the missing capability for
region-confined previews is control, and that control can live entirely on the
client.
